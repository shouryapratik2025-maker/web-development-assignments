Space Exploration

From the first artificial satellite in 1957 to modern Mars rovers, humanity's journey into space has been defined by bold leaps of science and courage.

Major Milestones — A Chronological Timeline

The following events represent the most significant achievements in the history of space exploration, listed in chronological order:

  1. 1957 — Sputnik 1 (USSR): The world's first artificial satellite, launched on 4 October 1957. It completed one orbit every 96.2 minutes and transmitted radio signals back to Earth, shocking the Western world and triggering the Space Race.
  2. 1961 — Yuri Gagarin (USSR): On 12 April 1961, cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin became the first human in space, completing one orbit of Earth aboard Vostok 1 in 108 minutes.
  3. 1969 — Apollo 11 Moon Landing (USA): On 20 July 1969, Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first humans to walk on the Moon. Armstrong's words — "one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind" — were broadcast live to millions.
  4. 1971 — Mariner 9 (USA): Became the first spacecraft to orbit another planet (Mars), mapping 85% of the Martian surface and discovering Olympus Mons.
  5. 1977 — Voyager 1 & 2 (USA): Launched to explore the outer Solar System. Voyager 1 is now the farthest human-made object, over 23 billion km from Earth.
  6. 1990 — Hubble Space Telescope (USA/ESA): Deployed into low Earth orbit, Hubble has produced some of the most detailed images of distant galaxies, nebulae, and exoplanets.
  7. 1997 — Mars Pathfinder & Sojourner (USA): The first successful Mars rover, Sojourner, explored the Martian surface for 83 days, proving rovers were viable for planetary science.
  8. 2004 — Cassini–Huygens Arrives at Saturn (USA/ESA): Cassini spent 13 years orbiting Saturn, discovering active geysers on Enceladus and detailing Saturn's ring structure in unprecedented clarity.
  9. 2012 — Curiosity Rover lands on Mars (USA): Curiosity's car-sized rover landed in Gale Crater using a revolutionary sky-crane system. It confirmed Mars once had liquid water.
  10. 2021 — James Webb Space Telescope Launched (USA/ESA/CSA): JWST began observing in 2022, providing the deepest infrared images of the universe ever captured, observing galaxies formed just 300 million years after the Big Bang.

Major Space Agencies

National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)

Founded in 1958 by the United States government, NASA is the world's most well-known space agency. It has been responsible for the Apollo, Voyager, Hubble, and Mars Rover programs, among hundreds of others.

European Space Agency (ESA)

ESA is an intergovernmental organisation of 22 member states founded in 1975. Notable missions include the Rosetta comet orbiter and the Huygens probe that landed on Saturn's moon Titan.

ISRO — Indian Space Research Organisation

India's national space agency, founded in 1969, achieved global recognition with Chandrayaan-3 in 2023, successfully landing near the Moon's south pole — a first for any space agency.

Note: Other major agencies include Roscosmos (Russia), CNSA (China), and JAXA (Japan), all of which have made significant contributions to space science.

Upcoming & Future Missions

The next decade of space exploration promises even bolder ventures. Here is an unordered list of planned missions:

  • NASA Artemis Programme: Return humans to the Moon, including the first woman and first person of colour to walk on the lunar surface.
  • Mars Sample Return (NASA/ESA): Retrieve rock samples collected by Perseverance rover and bring them back to Earth.
  • Europa Clipper (NASA): Perform detailed reconnaissance of Jupiter's moon Europa, which may harbour a liquid ocean beneath its icy crust.
  • ISRO Gaganyaan: India's first crewed orbital mission, aiming to send a 3-member crew to a 400 km orbit for up to 3 days.
