10 Amazing Solar System Facts

Here are ten fascinating facts about our Solar System, drawn from decades of scientific research:

  • The Solar System is estimated to be 4.568 billion years old, about one-third the age of the Universe itself.
  • The Sun contains 99.86% of all the mass in the Solar System.
  • Jupiter's moon Ganymede is larger than the planet Mercury.
  • A day on Venus (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days) — it rotates more slowly than it orbits the Sun.
  • Saturn is less dense than water — it would float if you put it in a bathtub large enough.
  • The asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter contains millions of rocky objects, but its total mass is less than 4% of the Moon's.
  • Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) in 2006.
  • Olympus Mons on Mars is the tallest volcano in the Solar System, standing about 21.9 km high — nearly 3× the height of Mount Everest.
  • The Voyager 1 probe, launched in 1977, entered interstellar space in 2012, becoming the first human-made object to leave the Solar System.
  • There may be a hypothetical Planet Nine — a large undetected planet in the far outer Solar System, suggested by anomalies in the orbits of distant objects.

Layers of the Sun

The Sun has several distinct structural layers:

Interior Layers

The Sun's interior, invisible from outside, generates all of its energy through nuclear fusion.

The Core

The innermost region, where temperatures reach 15 million °C and hydrogen fuses into helium, releasing enormous energy.

Radiative Zone

Energy travels outward through this layer as electromagnetic radiation, taking up to 100,000 years to cross it.

Convective Zone

Hot plasma rises, cools, and sinks back down in convection currents, transferring energy toward the surface.

Outer Layers

Photosphere

The visible surface of the Sun, at approximately 5,778 K. Sunspots — cooler, dark regions — form here.

Chromosphere

A reddish layer above the photosphere, visible during solar eclipses. Solar flares originate here.

Corona

The outermost layer — a scorching plasma halo reaching temperatures of over 1 million K. It is the source of the solar wind.
