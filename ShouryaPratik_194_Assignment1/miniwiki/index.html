What is the Solar System?

The Solar System is the gravitationally bound system of the Sun and the objects that orbit it. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant interstellar molecular cloud.

At its centre lies the Sun, a G-type main-sequence star that accounts for 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System. The remaining mass consists of eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and interplanetary dust.

Fig 1: The eight planets of the Solar System, to scale. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

Key Fact: Light from the Sun takes approximately 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach Earth, travelling at 299,792 km/s.

The Eight Planets

The Solar System contains eight officially recognised planets, divided into two groups:

Inner (Terrestrial) Planets

Rocky, small, and close to the Sun:

  • Mercury — Closest to the Sun; no atmosphere.
  • Venus — Hottest planet; thick CO₂ atmosphere.
  • Earth — Only known planet to harbour life.
  • Mars — The “Red Planet”; home of Olympus Mons, the tallest volcano.

Outer (Gas/Ice Giant) Planets

Massive, gaseous, and far from the Sun:

  • Jupiter — Largest planet; famous for its Great Red Spot.
  • Saturn — Distinguished by its stunning ring system.
  • Uranus — Rotates on its side; an ice giant.
  • Neptune — Farthest planet; has the fastest winds in the Solar System.

The Sun — Heart of the Solar System

The Sun is a nearly perfect sphere of hot plasma, internally convecting with energy produced by nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium in its core.

Core Physical Properties

Diameter: 1,392,700 km (about 109× Earth's diameter)
Surface Temperature: 5,778 K
Age: ~4.603 billion years
Spectral Class: G2V (Yellow Dwarf)
